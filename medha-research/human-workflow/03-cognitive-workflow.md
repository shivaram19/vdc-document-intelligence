# Cognitive Workflow: How Experts Think When Reading Drawings

## Expert Pattern Recognition

Research on expertise and visual search confirms that experienced construction professionals process drawings differently from novices:

### Holistic Processing
- Experts see **assemblies and systems** rather than individual lines
- A veteran PE sees a "wall assembly" where a junior sees "lines and hatches"
- They mentally group elements into functional systems (HVAC zone, structural bay, electrical circuit)

### Anomaly Detection
- Experts are tuned to detect deviations from expected patterns
- Incorrect line weights, missing dimensions, or conflicting symbols jump out immediately
- They have a "mental database" of what "looks right" based on thousands of past drawings

### Cross-Referencing Automation
- Experts automatically trigger mental cross-checks
- When they see "D-101" on a plan, they mentally flag: door schedule → hardware set → section reference → detail reference
- This happens without conscious effort — it's pattern-matching at scale

### Usefulness Filtering
- Experts allocate attention based on task relevance
- An estimator scanning for quantities notices count symbols
- A coordinator scanning for clashes notices spatial overlaps
- A preconstruction director scanning for risk notices code compliance gaps

## The Systematic Reading Process

### Step 1: Title Block First
- Verify project name, sheet number, scale, revision date
- **Red flag**: Working from an outdated revision

### Step 2: Legend and General Notes
- Decode symbols and catch special instructions
- Notes outside the title block supersede title block information

### Step 3: Orientation
- North arrow, gridlines, section cut markers
- Establish spatial reference frame

### Step 4: The "Bones"
- Walls, columns, structural grid, major openings
- Understand the skeleton before the systems

### Step 5: Systems Overlay
- MEP symbols, ceiling heights, finish tags
- Map systems onto the structural framework

### Step 6: Detail Chasing
- Follow reference markers to enlarged plans and sections
- "See Detail A/A3" → find A3 sheet → read detail → cross-reference back

### Step 7: Cross-Discipline Verification
- Check at least two other disciplines for every significant element
- Architectural wall type vs. structural support vs. MEP routing

## Red Flags That Jump Out to Experts

### Critical Red Flags
1. **"NTS" (Not To Scale)** on detail drawings — never scale from these
2. Dimensions that don't add up across a bay or building edge
3. Missing structural support under heavy equipment
4. MEP risers that don't align floor-to-floor
5. Sprinkler heads drawn over light fixtures or HVAC diffusers
6. Door swings that conflict with adjacent door swings or equipment
7. Missing fire ratings on partitions between occupancy types
8. Rebar shown too congested for concrete placement
9. Panel schedules that don't match floor plan circuiting

### Subtle Red Flags
- **"Typical"** notes that may mean "repeat everywhere" on one project but "coordinate per plan" on another
- Scale verification failures: 20% of digital plans import at wrong scale
- Addenda items contractors miss that completely alter quantities
- Fine-print specification requirements buried in 70-page submittals
- Cross-trade interface issues that don't show up in coarse clash detection tolerances

## The "Mental Database"

Experienced coordinators carry an enormous implicit knowledge base:
- Code minimums (ceiling heights, corridor widths, stair dimensions)
- Typical construction sequences (structure first, then MEP, then finishes)
- Manufacturer standard sizes (door heights, window modules, panelboard dimensions)
- Common detailing patterns (how a wall meets a ceiling, how a beam connects to a column)
- Jurisdiction-specific requirements (local amendments to IBC, NEC, NFPA)

This is why junior PEs miss things that seniors catch instantly — they haven't built the mental database yet.
