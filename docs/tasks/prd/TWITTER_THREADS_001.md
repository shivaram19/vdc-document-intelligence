## Thread Option 2: Problem-First — The Hidden Cost of Document Contradictions

*Tone: Sharp, data-driven, industry-specific. Best for construction-tech audience.*

1/ Construction projects are drowning in contradictions.

A spec says one fire rating. A drawing says another. An addendum updates one but not the other. The RFI answer conflicts with the bulletin.

By the time someone catches it, concrete is being poured.

🧵

2/ Rework costs 5–15% of total project value.

25–50% of that rework traces back to design and documentation errors.

On a $50M project, that's $2.5M–$7.5M in avoidable cost. On a $500M project, it's catastrophic.

3/ The current fix? Human reviewers reading hundreds or thousands of pages, cross-referencing specs, drawings, addenda, RFIs, and submittals.

It works until it doesn't. Humans miss things. Versions drift. Deadlines compress.

4/ Most "AI for construction" products are search boxes.

Ask a question, get a paragraph. Useful, but passive.

The real value is preventive: catch contradictions before they become change orders, delays, and disputes.

5/ That's what I'm building with Medha.

We treat construction documents as a graph of commitments:
- Owner requirements → specs
- Specs → drawings
- Drawings → submittals
- Submittals → RFIs
- RFIs → addenda → revised drawings/specs

When one node changes, we check the others.

6/ The approach:
- Extract parametric claims from documents
- Build a version-aware claim graph
- Detect conflicts, omissions, and version mismatches
- Surface findings ranked by severity and confidence
- Let human experts adjudicate

7/ It's not about replacing architects, engineers, or VDC coordinators.

It's about giving them a tireless first-pass reviewer that never forgets a previous version and never gets tired at page 900.

8/ The bigger bet: this becomes part of an agentic construction workflow.

Agents monitor documents. Agents flag drift. Humans decide.

That's the future of construction administration I want to build.

9/ I'm talking to VDC coordinators, BIM managers, and GC project engineers every week.

If you work in construction and deal with specs/drawings/RFIs/submittals, I'd love to learn from your workflow.

DMs open.

10/ We're building in public.

Research, architecture, customer discovery notes — all shared.

If construction document intelligence interests you, follow along.

3/ The other half is the company itself.

I'm a solo founder. I don't want to become a manager of contractors. I want to build an agentic enterprise: a company where agents handle execution and humans handle intent.

4/ Not automation. Automation follows rules.

Agentic systems pursue goals, use tools, adapt to context, and know when to ask for help.

The founder sets direction. Agents move toward it. Humans stay in the loop for anything that touches people, money, or commitments.

5/ Why open source?

Because agentic companies should be inspectable.
Because the best infrastructure should be reusable.
Because "move fast and break things" is not acceptable when agents can act on your behalf.

- Plane for work and decisions
- n8n for orchestration
- MCP for agent-tool connections
- Langfuse/Laminar for observability

A wrong spec interpretation can mean a failed inspection, a change order, or unsafe work.

So our agents don't just answer questions. They surface contradictions, cite sources, and hand off to human experts for decisions.

## LinkedIn Version: Option 2 (Problem-First Construction)

*Tone: Professional, insight-driven, single long-form post. Best for LinkedIn construction-tech and VDC audiences.*

---

Construction projects are drowning in contradictions.

A spec says one fire rating. A drawing says another. An addendum updates one document but not the other. The RFI answer conflicts with a later bulletin.

By the time someone catches it, concrete is being poured.

The numbers are sobering.

Rework costs 5–15% of total project value. And 25–50% of that rework traces back to design and documentation errors.

On a $50M project, that's $2.5M–$7.5M in avoidable cost. On a $500M project, the scale becomes catastrophic.

The current defense? Skilled people reviewing hundreds or thousands of pages, cross-referencing specs, drawings, addenda, RFIs, and submittals.

It works until it doesn't. Humans miss things. Versions drift. Deadlines compress attention.

Most "AI for construction" products today are better search boxes. Ask a question, get a paragraph. Useful, but passive.

The real value is preventive: catch contradictions before they become change orders, delays, and disputes.

That's what I'm building with Medha.

We treat construction documents as a graph of commitments:
→ Owner requirements flow into specs
→ Specs flow into drawings
→ Drawings flow into submittals
→ Submittals raise RFIs
→ RFIs lead to addenda
→ Addenda revise specs and drawings

When one node changes, we check the others.

Our approach:
• Extract parametric claims from text, drawings, tables, and markups
• Build a version-aware claim graph
• Detect conflicts, omissions, and version mismatches
• Rank findings by severity and confidence
• Hand off to human experts for adjudication

This is not about replacing architects, engineers, or VDC coordinators.

It's about giving them a tireless first-pass reviewer that never forgets a previous version and never gets tired at page 900.

The bigger bet is that this becomes part of an agentic construction workflow: agents monitor documents, agents flag drift, humans decide.

I'm talking to VDC coordinators, BIM managers, and GC project engineers every week to understand real workflows.

If you work in construction and deal with specs, drawings, RFIs, or submittals, I'd love to learn from your experience.
